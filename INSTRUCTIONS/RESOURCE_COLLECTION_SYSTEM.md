# RESOURCE COLLECTION SYSTEM
**How we choose what to read and watch.** The goal is *not* a giant library. The goal is the *smallest* set of resources that gives the *most* understanding.

`Part of: PROJECT ASI — Living Instruction System`
`System Version: 1.0 · Status: Living document · Last updated: 2026-06-20`

---

## 1. The one metric that matters

> **Understanding gained per hour invested.**

Not pages read. Not papers collected. Not videos saved. A 200-page book that rewires how you see a field beats 50 papers you skim and forget.

**Anti-goal:** the giant unread library. A list of 300 "important" papers you'll never read is not knowledge — it's anxiety with a table of contents.

---

## 2. The resource ladder (usually best first)

For a *new* field, the fastest path to real understanding is usually:

1. **One great overview** — a book, long article, or lecture series by someone who can *explain*, not just *know*. (Builds the map.)
2. **One or two landmark primary sources** — the actual paper/result that changed the field. (Touches the real thing.)
3. **A strong critic** — someone smart who thinks the field is *wrong*. (Reveals the assumptions.)
4. **A practitioner's view** — someone who *builds* in the field, not just theorizes. (Reveals what really matters in practice.)

Four well-chosen resources can give a working understanding of most fields. Add more only when a specific question demands it.

---

## 3. How to judge a resource *before* spending hours on it

- **Explanatory power** — does the author make hard things clear, or just sound impressive?
- **Signal density** — insights per page. (Some famous books are 90% padding.)
- **Primacy** — is this the source, or a copy of a copy?
- **Honesty** — does it admit uncertainty and show the other side?
- **Durability** — will this still matter in 5 years, or is it this month's hype?
- **Fit to the question** — the best resource *in general* is not always the best for *what we're asking now*.

---

## 4. Types of high-leverage resources (in rough order)

Books · survey/review papers · landmark primary papers · lecture series & courses · deep interviews & debates (especially *disagreements* between experts) · well-chosen blog posts/essays by serious practitioners · historical case studies (what actually happened, and why predictions failed or succeeded).

**Debates and disagreements are gold** — watching two experts who *disagree* reason against each other exposes the real assumptions faster than ten agreeing summaries.

---

## 5. Guard against the staleness trap

Some knowledge ages fast (current AI capabilities, who's ahead, latest results). Some ages slowly (math, how brains work, thermodynamics, history). 

- For **fast-aging** topics, prefer recent sources and check the date; the assistant's knowledge may be out of date, so verify against current information when it matters.
- For **slow-aging** topics, the classics are often still the best.

Always note a resource's **date** and whether the topic is fast- or slow-aging.

---

## 6. What we record for each resource

When a resource earns its place, it gets a short entry (later under `RESOURCES/`):
- title, author, year, type, fast/slow-aging;
- why chosen (which question/domain it serves);
- leverage estimate;
- status (queued / processing / processed);
- link to the understanding-note produced from it (see [`KNOWLEDGE_PROCESSING_SYSTEM.md`](KNOWLEDGE_PROCESSING_SYSTEM.md)).

A resource isn't "done" when it's read. It's done when it's been *processed into understanding* and connected to the maps.
