# RESEARCH METHOD
**How we investigate a question.** A repeatable recipe so investigations stay rigorous instead of random.

`Part of: PROJECT ASI — Living Instruction System`
`System Version: 1.0 · Status: Living document · Last updated: 2026-06-20`

---

## 1. Start with a sharp question

A vague question gives a vague answer. We sharpen first.

- **Vague:** "How does AGI work?"
- **Sharp:** "What specific abilities does a human child have by age 4 that today's best AI systems still lack — and why?"

A good question is **specific**, **answerable in principle**, and **decision-relevant** (knowing the answer would change what we think or do).

---

## 2. The investigation recipe

```
1. FRAME      What exactly are we asking? Why does it matter? What would
              count as a good answer?
2. DECOMPOSE  Break the big question into smaller sub-questions.
3. MAP KNOWNS State what we already believe, and how confident we are.
4. GATHER     Pull the highest-leverage evidence (see RESOURCE_COLLECTION).
5. STEEL-MAN  Build the strongest version of each competing answer.
6. WEIGH      Compare evidence, assumptions, trade-offs. Tag confidence.
7. CONCLUDE   State a current best answer — clearly revisable.
8. EXPOSE     List what's still unknown → feed OPEN_QUESTIONS_SYSTEM.
9. PREDICT    Where possible, make a checkable prediction we could be
              wrong about. (This is how reality talks back to us.)
```

Steps 5, 8, and 9 are the ones people skip. We don't.

---

## 3. Evidence quality ladder (strongest → weakest)

1. **Repeated, independent results** (many groups, same finding).
2. **A single strong study or working system** you can inspect.
3. **Expert consensus** *with stated reasons* (the reasons matter, not the consensus).
4. **A single expert's opinion.**
5. **Analogy or intuition.**
6. **"Everyone knows…"** (treat as a red flag, not evidence).

Always note which rung a claim sits on. Most disagreements come from one side standing on rung 5 while thinking it's on rung 1.

---

## 4. Separating the three layers of any claim

To avoid confusion, split every claim into:

- **Fact** — what was observed/measured. (Most solid.)
- **Interpretation** — what people think the fact *means*. (Less solid.)
- **Projection** — what people think it implies for the future. (Least solid.)

Example: *Fact* — a model's score went up when made bigger. *Interpretation* — "bigger models understand more." *Projection* — "so scaling leads to AGI." Each layer is shakier than the last. Keeping them separate prevents a solid fact from smuggling in a shaky projection.

---

## 5. Reference-class thinking for predictions

When estimating "how long until X" or "how likely is Y," first ask: **what is the track record of similar predictions?** Past forecasts of "AGI in N years," "fusion in N years," "self-driving next year" have a *pattern*. We learn from the pattern, not just the current excitement. (This guards against both hype and dismissal.)

---

## 6. Output of every investigation

Each finished investigation produces a short, structured note:
- the question,
- the current best answer + confidence tag,
- the strongest opposing view,
- the key evidence used,
- what's still unknown,
- (if possible) one falsifiable prediction.

These notes are the raw material the rest of the system maps, teaches, and improves.
