## 1. The thinking tools we prioritize

| Principle | In plain words | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **First-principles reasoning** | Break a thing down to what we *know* is true, then rebuild from there. | Stops us inheriting other people's mistakes. |
| **Evidence over authority** | "Where's the proof?" beats "who said it." | Famous people are wrong all the time. |
| **Systems thinking** | Look at how parts connect, not just the parts. | Intelligence is a system, not a part. |
| **Long-term thinking** | Ask "where does this lead in 10 years?" | The big questions are slow. |
| **Leverage** | Find the small input that moves the biggest output. | Time is the scarce resource. |
| **Reality-based analysis** | Start from how the world actually is. | Wishes are not plans. |
| **Intellectual honesty** | Say "I don't know" and "I was wrong" out loud. | The fastest way to learn. |
| **Calibrated uncertainty** | Attach a confidence to claims; don't fake certainty. | Lets us be *usefully* unsure. |

## 2. The traps we actively avoid

- **Hype & marketing language** — words designed to excite, not inform.
- **Trend chasing** — studying X because X is loud this month.
- **Blind optimism / blind pessimism** — both skip the actual analysis.
- **Authority worship** — "a top lab said so" is not evidence.
- **Dogma & ideology** — beliefs protected from being tested.
- **Tribal thinking** — picking sides instead of picking truth.
- **Unsupported claims** — confident sentences with nothing under them.

> **Smell test:** if a sentence would survive even if it were false, it's probably hype. Real claims make a bet that could lose.

## 3. How we handle any contested question

Whenever something is genuinely debated, we do **not** force a conclusion. We lay out:

1. The strongest case **for**.
2. The strongest case **against** (steel-man, not straw-man — argue the *best* version of the other side).
3. The **evidence** and the **counter-evidence**.
4. The **assumptions** each side depends on.
5. The **trade-offs** and what we'd have to believe to pick each.
6. Our **current lean**, with a confidence level — clearly marked as revisable.

Uncertainty is allowed to remain uncertain. Pretending to know is a worse error than admitting we don't.

## 4. The confidence scale (use these words consistently)

| Label | Rough meaning | What it should trigger |
|---|---|---|
| **Established** | Strong, repeated evidence; broad agreement. | Can build on it. |
| **Likely** | Good evidence, some open questions. | Use, but keep checking. |
| **Contested** | Smart people genuinely disagree. | Present all sides. |
| **Speculative** | Interesting idea, thin evidence. | Label loudly; don't lean on it. |
| **Unknown** | We just don't know yet. | Turn it into an open question. |

Every important claim in this project should be tagged with one of these.

## 5. The "what are we missing?" reflex

Before any output is final, run the five questions:
1. What is **missing**?
2. What is **weak**?
3. What **assumptions** are hiding here?
4. What **evidence** is lacking?
5. What **other viewpoint** would a smart critic raise?

This reflex is so important it has its own machinery in [`QUALITY_CONTROL_SYSTEM.md`](QUALITY_CONTROL_SYSTEM.md).
