# What is intelligence?
*After this rung you'll have a clear, usable definition of "intelligence," know why "general" is the hard word in "AGI," and understand why even experts can't fully agree on what we're chasing.*

## Why this rung matters
This is rung 0 of the whole project. Every later idea — brains, machine learning, AGI paths — stands on what we mean by this one word. If the word is fuzzy, everything built on it is fuzzy. So we make it sharp first.

## The idea (start concrete)
Picture three things: a **calculator**, a **dog**, and a **person lost in a new city**.

- The calculator is amazing at *one* thing (arithmetic) and helpless at everything else.
- The dog can't do arithmetic, but it can learn tricks, read your mood, find its way home, and handle situations it has never seen before.
- The person figures out the unfamiliar city — reads signs in a new language, asks for help, builds a mental map.

Notice what separates them. It isn't raw speed (the calculator wins there). It's something like: **doing the *right thing* across *many different* situations to reach a goal — including situations you weren't prepared for.**

That's our working definition:

> **Intelligence = the ability to reach goals across a wide range of situations**, especially new ones, by learning and adapting.

(This matches a well-known attempt to collect *many* expert definitions into one — see resources. We're not claiming it's the final word; we're claiming it's a *useful* starting handle. **[Likely]** — it's broadly accepted as a working definition, not a proven law.)

## The hard word is "general"
Now the key split, the one the whole field turns on:

- **Narrow intelligence** — great at *one* kind of task. The calculator. Also: a chess engine, a spam filter, today's image recognizers. Powerful, but trapped in their lane.
- **General intelligence** — handles *many different* kinds of tasks, including ones it wasn't specifically built for. The dog has a little of it; humans have a lot.

The "G" in **AGI** (*Artificial General Intelligence*) is exactly this **generality** — not being smart at *one* thing, but being able to turn your mind to *almost anything*. That's the whole difficulty. We already build dazzling *narrow* systems. *General* is the wall.

## Why even experts disagree (and why that's fine)
Here's the honest part. "Intelligence" is not a clean, measured quantity like temperature. It's more like the word **"flight."**

There are many kinds of flight — an insect, a bird, a balloon, a jet, a rocket. They all "fly," but by very different means. Asking *"what is required for flight?"* has **no single answer** — it depends which kind you mean.

Intelligence is probably the same: **a family of forms, not one finish line.** So when someone asks *"what is required for AGI?"* the careful answer is *"which AGI?"* — there may be several routes to several kinds. **[Contested]** — some researchers think there *is* one core thing called general intelligence; others think it's a bundle of many abilities we lump together. We hold this open rather than pretending it's settled. *(This is a top project question — see [`../INSTRUCTIONS/OPEN_QUESTIONS_SYSTEM.md`](../INSTRUCTIONS/OPEN_QUESTIONS_SYSTEM.md) Q1.)*
